The Great Melbourne Shift

Melbourne's growth is no longer centered on the inner city. Since the pandemic, internal migration has shifted outwards, with outer suburban and regional areas gaining residents while inner Melbourne records significant losses.

PART 2: TEMPORARY OR STRUCTURAL?

Is the shift temporary?

A single year only shows part of the story. Population trends show that inner Melbourne declined before beginning to recover, while Melbourne - West and Geelong continued to grow. Comparing 2021 with 2025 also shows how internal migration losses and gains intensified after the pandemic period.

Colours represent selected focus regions. The population chart is indexed to 2019, while the migration chart compares 2021 with 2025 internal migration gain or loss.

This is not just a one-year shock. Inner Melbourne has recovered in population, but internal migration losses have intensified.

Population scale and density reveal different pressures

Dense inner areas are not always the strongest population gainers. Melbourne - Inner has high density but the largest internal migration loss, while larger outer regions such as Melbourne - West absorb more residents overall.

PART 3: WHO IS MOVING?

Who is driving the shift?

The movement outward is not only about places. Age patterns help show which groups are associated with inner, outer and regional growth. Different regions attract different age groups. Inner Melbourne remains comparatively younger, with a higher concentration of younger adults, while outer suburban and regional and coastal areas are dominated by family-age populations and older residents. The heatmap shows how age groups are distributed across selected regions.

Key age insight

Inner Melbourne has the strongest working-age profile, while Latrobe–Gippsland, Mornington Peninsula and Bendigo have older age structures. This helps explain why migration patterns differ across inner, outer and regional areas.

What drives population change?

Population growth is not caused by migration alone. Different regions are shaped by different combinations of natural increase, internal migration, and overseas migration.

Overseas migration offsets large internal migration losses, allowing Inner Melbourne's population to recover despite continued resident outflow.

Housing affordability and remote work are reshaping Melbourne

The outward shift is not only about population numbers. Inner Melbourne combines high housing costs, higher work-from-home rates and strong public transport use, while outer and regional growth areas are generally more affordable and more car-dependent. This suggests people are choosing different trade-offs between cost, space, work, and mobility.

Higher work-from-home rates coincide with stronger migration gains in outer regions.

PART 4: What does this mean?

Melbourne’s growth is becoming increasingly decentralised.

The evidence across migration, housing, work, and age patterns suggests that Melbourne’s post-pandemic growth is becoming increasingly decentralised. While inner Melbourne continues to attract overseas migrants and younger adults, many residents are moving toward outer suburban and regional areas offering greater affordability and different lifestyles.

Regions such as Melbourne – West, Geelong, and the Mornington Peninsula are no longer peripheral growth areas, they are becoming central to Melbourne’s future population growth.

These patterns suggest that future infrastructure, housing and transport planning will need to focus increasingly on outer suburban and regional growth corridors.
